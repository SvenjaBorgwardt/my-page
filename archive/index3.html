The Idea

Large language models are trained to be helpful, and in the context of learning, that is not always what is best for the person. That is why I started fine-tuning models to realign what helpful means when someone is trying to learn.

01
Helpfulness is the problem.
A general-purpose model is trained to be helpful, so it gives you the most complete, most polished answer it can. That is exactly wrong when someone is trying to learn. For something to be learned, it has to be slightly harder than easy. AI makes everything feel effortless, and students mistake that feeling for understanding.
02
Condition the help on the student's effort, not on the question.
So I am training models to gauge how much they help by the effort the student actually puts in, instead of just answering the question they asked. I call it Effort Conditioned Helpfulness. A model that talks to a struggling student differently than to one who is almost there, and helps just enough to push them forward without doing the thinking for them.
03
Most of what is called AI in education is the wrong bet.
Faster grading. Chatbots that answer student questions. Lesson-plan generators. All of it takes the current structure as given and just tries to run it more efficiently. The system we have was designed for scale, not for individuals. That was never the goal. It was a constraint.
04
This is a fine-tuning problem, not a prompting problem.
Making AI less helpful is harder than making it more helpful, and I do not think prompting will ever fully solve it. The model needs to learn what good teaching actually looks like: creating the conditions for someone to find the answer themselves.

Each project below is an attempt at this, from a different angle. Here is what the idea looks like in practice.

Student

“I don't understand why central banks raise interest rates during inflation. Higher rates make loans more expensive, so how does that help people afford things?”

Standard AI

“Higher rates cool demand by making borrowing more expensive. Less demand against the same supply slows price increases. That's how rate hikes fight inflation.”

Socratic

“What is inflation? What causes prices to rise? What role does demand play? How do interest rates affect consumer behaviour?”

Effort Conditioned

“You're right that loans get more expensive, that's exactly the plan. Think about it: if saving suddenly earns you 4% and a loan costs 6%, what do you do, buy the new car on credit now, or wait? And if lots of people do the same, what happens to demand? And to prices, when fewer people want to buy?”

Same question, three ways to answer it. Only the third takes the student's own reasoning as the starting point.

How I'm Testing It
Where the idea started
Compass & Debate Dojo

Helpfulness is great, just not when someone is trying to learn. Compass is a writing tool built to prevent the illusion of understanding students get when the AI has done the thinking for them. I had to handcuff the model by hand, hardcoding every learning step so it helps just enough. Debate Dojo applies the same thinking to debating, and is where I first fine-tuned a model to do this instead of hardcoding it.

LinksCompass, live · Debate Dojo
Realigning what “helpful” means
GemmPen

I fine-tuned Gemma 4 to read handwritten exams, evaluate them against my rubric, and write individual feedback with exercises built around each student's actual mistakes. Any large model can grade, so that is not why I am fine-tuning. I want to change what the model optimises for. It runs entirely on-device and learns from my corrections.

StackGemma 4 + LoRA · on-device · retrains from teacher corrections

Helpfulness by disappearing
UTE

A voice-first register for bakery counters. It listens while the assistant talks to the customer and builds the order in the background, so the conversation never has to stop. The same instinct, inverted: what if the best way for an AI to be useful is to disappear? First place at the BÄKO Hackathon.

The infrastructure underneath
Claudia

My multi-agent infrastructure, running on an always-on Mac Mini in my flat. By day she orchestrates the agents behind my student feedback, the rest of the time she runs my home automation. Most of what I know about agent design I learned by building her and then watching her break.
