Station 01 / intro

I've been spending a lot of time on a problem that might sound simple but isn't.

Large language models are trained to be helpful, and in the context of learning, this is not always what is best for the person. That's why I started fine-tuning models to realign what helpful means when someone is trying to learn.

Station 03 / project no. 01

Kaleido — feedback that lands.

A tool for giving individual feedback on writing. With hundreds of papers a week, proper individual feedback simply wasn't possible. Kaleido is what I made instead.

A paper comes in. The name is stripped locally, replaced with a code. An anonymisation agent removes every remaining personal detail. A set of agents analyses the work along separate dimensions — structure, evidence, language, coherence, grammar, content. A final agent writes the feedback, addressed to the writer rather than about them.

I called it Kaleido because a kaleidoscope turns the same fragments into a different pattern every time you look.

"Students came back with questions about their own essays. Not about the mark. About the essay itself."

Station 04 / project no. 02

Compass — direction, not destination.

AI creates a specific failure of self-assessment: a writer reads the quality of the output as evidence of their own learning. The illusion of fluency.

For something to be learned, it has to be slightly harder than easy — Robert Bjork's desirable difficulties. Socratic agents are the obvious counter-move. They annoy actual learners. I'm fine-tuning a small model to sit between the two.

example · a coding bug
learner:"my code errors."
standard:"missing a colon."
what i want:"read the error again. look at line 1."
A compass points the way. It doesn't hand you the map.

Station 05 / project no. 03

UTE — an AI that disappears.

A voice-first ordering system for bakery counters. Built in a weekend at the BÄKO Hackathon with three other women. We won first place.

The assistant keeps talking to the customer. UTE listens, transcribes, matches against the catalogue, builds the order silently. When the assistant repeats the order back — which she'd do anyway — that serves as confirmation. Nothing gets clicked.

What if the best way for an AI to be useful is to disappear?

Station 06 / also built

Claudia & Investor Game.

A multi-agent infrastructure always on in my flat, and a classroom platform where play-money meets pitch-quality.

Claudia runs locally on a Mac Mini. By day she orchestrates the agents behind Kaleido. By night she runs my home automation. Most of what I know about agents I learned from watching her break in interesting ways.

Investor Game — a platform for peer pitch + play-money investment. I wrote it because nothing off the shelf handled the combination. I've never seen market economics taken more seriously.
