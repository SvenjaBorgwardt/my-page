i.
Kaleido, or: on feedback that lands
Project No. 01
Feb 2025 → now

Kaleido is a tool for giving individual feedback on student writing. It came out of a frustration I had no answer for: with 240 students a week, proper individual feedback on written work was simply not possible. I started vibe coding in early 2025, and this was my first big project.1

What's left is a GDPR and EU AI Act compliant pipeline that gives individual feedback to my students and generates targeted exercises for the specific mistakes they make, so they can actually improve.

(carefully) yes A student hands in their paper. Their name is stripped out locally and replaced with a code. An anonymisation agent removes every remaining personal detail. A set of agents analyses the work along separate dimensions — structure, evidence, language, coherence, grammar, content. A final agent writes the feedback, addressed to the student rather than about them.

Students came to me afterwards with questions about their own essays. Not about the mark. About the essay itself.

They'd read the feedback because it was addressed to them, in language that treated them as the authors of something real, and they wanted to know more. They finally knew what to change, instead of just seeing where they'd gone wrong.2

addendum · 18 April 2026 looked back at the first Kaleido output last night — it's embarrassing. I trusted the model to handle tone. Now there's a dedicated "addressed-ness" agent. rev 03 next week if I can keep the debate class quiet for two hours.

I called it Kaleido because a kaleidoscope turns the same fragments into a different pattern every time you look. That's what I want feedback to do: take a student's writing and let them see it from an angle they couldn't see on their own.

WHAT I TRIED AND CUT
  • A plagiarism-detection module (three months, proud of it, wrong problem)
  • A "progress heat map" on the student dashboard
  • An LLM-written mark suggestion (never reliable enough to hand a student)
  • Push notifications when feedback was ready
→ the subtraction was the hard part.
  1. I didn't know what vibe coding was until I was already doing it. Had I known, I might have been more self-conscious about it.
  2. Mistral specifically — because EU jurisdiction matters when you're handling German school data. I'd choose them again. Probably.

ii.
Compass, or: on direction
Project No. 02
Jun 2025 → now

Watching how people use AI, I keep noticing one thing: people tend to take the path of least resistance. In learning contexts, that worries me. AI creates a specific failure of self-assessment: the student produces a good output and reads the quality of the output as evidence of their own learning. Cognitive science calls this the illusion of fluency.3

For something to be learned, it has to be slightly harder than easy — Robert Bjork's desirable difficulties. What I see in the classroom is the opposite: students handing in well-written AI work they didn't understand themselves.

Socratic agents are the obvious counter-move. I've tried them, and if you just want to understand a concept, getting questions back is annoying. At least for some tasks, it makes learning more frustrating, not less.

Example · A coding bug
Student:"My Python code doesn't work: for i in range(10) print(i)"
Standard:"You're missing a colon. Here's the fix."
Socratic:"What is a for loop? What is syntax?"
What I want:"Read the error message again, then look at the end of line 1."

So I started building a tool that removes the AI's ability to give the answer directly. I tried to handcuff the model into being helpful. Then I scalpelled each learning step into a hardcoded process that only helps at a very low level. That made the whole thing hard to transfer.

second thought · 03 April 2026 I think the whole handcuff frame was wrong. The model should want to help less, not be prevented from helping. Fine-tuning is closer to asking a patient colleague to let the student struggle for thirty seconds longer. That's what I'm testing now.

Right now I'm fine-tuning Mistral 7B with LoRA and DPO. The goal is to make it genuinely useful for my English classes — specifically for debating.

A compass points the way. It doesn't hand you the map.
  1. Robert A. Bjork, Desirable Difficulties. The older I get, the more I think this is the most important idea in education. I keep a photocopy of the 1994 paper in my desk drawer at school. It's dog-eared.

iii.
UTE — an AI that disappears
Project No. 03
BÄKO Hackathon · Nov 2025

UTE is a voice-first ordering system for bakery counters. I built the first version at the BÄKO Hackathon with three other women, and we won first place.

Germany has a long tradition in bakery handwerk. One of the last disciplines where the supermarket chains can't really compete, because the local bakery is something people are proud of. Especially in smaller towns, everyone has their shop — the one where the person behind the counter knows them by name. What holds these shops together is conversation. The assistant knows your family, your allergies, the cake last week was for your daughter. Then the order has to go into the register, and the conversation breaks. The assistant looks down, hunts through the touchscreen, asks the customer to repeat themselves. The warmth is gone, interrupted by technology.

UTE handles the admin so the conversation doesn't have to stop. The system listens while the assistant talks, transcribes in real time, matches items against the bakery's catalogue, and builds the order in the background. When the assistant repeats the order back to the customer, which she'd do anyway, that serves as confirmation. Nothing gets clicked.

What if the best way for an AI to be useful is to disappear?

The reason I could build this in a weekend is that I'd already spent months on the same structure in a different context — a voice pipeline that listens, transcribes, matches, summarises. Point it at a bakery counter, and you get UTE.

UTE stands for Unkomplizierte Theken-Eingabe — uncomplicated counter input. It's also a common German name, which fit what we wanted: a presence at the counter, not a system.

iv.
Also built
Smaller, still mine
2024 → now
Multi-agent · Mac Mini

Claudia

My multi-agent infrastructure. She runs locally on a Mac Mini that's always on in my flat. By day she orchestrates the agents behind Kaleido. The rest of the time she runs my home automation. I named her Claudia because "the multi-agent framework on the Mac Mini" stopped being charming around the tenth mention.

→ she also hosts Sarah (my smart home), after the bad sci-fi show Eureka.

Market economics

Investor Game

A platform where students develop product ideas, pitch to the group, and invest play money into each other's pitches. Scoring is part quality-of-pitch, part performance-of-portfolio. I wrote it because nothing off the shelf handled that combination. Deployed in my market economics class — I've never seen the topic taken more seriously.

Corrigenda, for the record
Things I got wrong
in earlier revisions
ERRATA · Rev. 01 → 02

In Rev. 01 of this page, I implied Kaleido's anonymisation was foolproof. It isn't — it currently fails on ~3% of names, specifically compound German surnames and non-Latin scripts. I am working on it. In the meantime, I re-check anonymised output by hand before feedback is sent.

In Rev. 01, I also described Compass as "Socratic." It isn't, and it shouldn't be. Corrected throughout.

The bakery receipt in § iii is, for the avoidance of doubt, a reconstruction. The original is taped above my desk.

Typeset in Source Serif 4 and JetBrains Mono, with marginalia in Caveat, Homemade Apple, and Shadows Into Light Two. Ornaments set in musical notation because I can't stop being a violinist. Red is reserved for things I would mark in red on a student's page: a real mistake, a real yes, an instruction to read again.

if a page can't show that a person wrote it, the person should write a different page.

Built by hand, revised often. Next revision when the debate class lets me.
