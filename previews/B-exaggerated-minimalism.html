No. 01 · Kaleido

Kaleido

A tool for giving individual feedback on student writing. With 240 students a week, proper individual feedback on written work was simply not possible. Kaleido is what I made instead.

A student hands in their paper. Their name is stripped out locally and replaced with a code. An anonymisation agent removes every remaining personal detail. A set of agents analyses the work along separate dimensions — structure, evidence, language, coherence, grammar, content. A final agent writes the feedback, addressed to the student rather than about them.

Students came to me afterwards with questions about their own essays. Not about the mark. About the essay itself.

Kaleido works for English and Economics because I trained the agents on my specific tasks. The whole thing is GDPR and EU AI Act compliant — which is the only reason I could actually use it on real student data.

No. 02 · Compass

Compass

Watching how people use AI, I keep noticing one thing: people tend to take the path of least resistance. In learning, that worries me.

AI creates a specific failure of self-assessment: the student produces a good output and reads the quality of the output as evidence of their own learning. Cognitive science calls this the illusion of fluency. For something to be learned, it has to be slightly harder than easy — Robert Bjork's desirable difficulties.

Example · A coding bug
Student"My code doesn't work: for i in range(10) print(i)"
Standard model"You're missing a colon. Here's the fix."
Socratic model"What is a for loop? What is syntax?"
What I want"Read the error message again, then look at the end of line 1."

Right now I'm fine-tuning Mistral 7B with LoRA and DPO. The goal is to make the model genuinely useful for my English classes, specifically for debating.

A compass points the way. It doesn't hand you the map.

No. 03 · UTE

UTE

A voice-first ordering system for bakery counters. Built at the BÄKO Hackathon with three other women. We won first place.

Germany's local bakery is a place of conversation. The assistant knows your family, your allergies, the cake last week was for your daughter. Then the order has to go into the register, and the conversation breaks. The warmth is gone, interrupted by technology.

UTE handles the admin so the conversation doesn't have to stop. The system listens while the assistant talks, transcribes in real time, matches items against the bakery's catalogue, and builds the order in the background. When the assistant repeats the order back to the customer, which she'd do anyway, that serves as confirmation. Nothing gets clicked.

What if the best way for an AI to be useful is to disappear?

UTE stands for Unkomplizierte Theken-Eingabe — uncomplicated counter input. It's also a common German name.
