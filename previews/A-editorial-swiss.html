Kaleido

A tool for giving individual feedback on student writing — the thing I had no answer for, with 240 students a week.

A student hands in their paper. Their name is stripped locally and replaced with a code. An anonymisation agent removes every remaining personal detail. A set of agents then analyses the work along separate dimensions: structure, evidence, language, coherence, grammar, content. A final agent writes the feedback, addressed to the student rather than about them.

The first time I handed back Kaleido feedback instead of the usual red-penned pages, students came to me afterwards with questions about their own essays. Not about the mark. About the essay itself.

The output is an HTML page they can read on their phone, with a progress view that shows how their writing has developed over the year. It works well for English and Economics because I trained the agents on my specific types of tasks. Publishing a version that fits other subjects is on my list.

A kaleidoscope turns the same fragments into a different pattern every time you look. That's what I want feedback to do.

Compass

Watching how people use AI, I keep noticing one thing: people tend to take the path of least resistance. In learning, that worries me.

AI creates a specific failure of self-assessment: the student produces a good output and reads the quality of the output as evidence of their own learning. Cognitive science calls this the illusion of fluency. For something to be learned, it has to be slightly harder than easy — Robert Bjork's desirable difficulties.

Example · A coding bug
Student: "My code doesn't work: for i in range(10) print(i)"
Standard model: "You're missing a colon."
Socratic model: "What is a for loop? What is syntax?"
What I want: "Read the error message again, then look at the end of line 1."

Socratic agents are the obvious counter-move. I've tried them, and honestly, if you just want to understand a concept, getting questions back is annoying. So I'm fine-tuning Mistral 7B with LoRA and DPO to make the model genuinely useful — without giving the answer outright.

A compass points the way. It doesn't hand you the map.

UTE — Unkomplizierte Theken-Eingabe

A voice-first ordering system for bakery counters. Built in a weekend at the BÄKO Hackathon with three other women. We won first place.

Germany's local bakery is a place where the assistant knows your family, your allergies, the cake last week was for your daughter. What holds it together is conversation. Then the order has to go into the register, and the conversation breaks.

UTE listens while the assistant talks. Transcribes in real time. Matches items against the bakery's catalogue. Builds the order in the background. When the assistant repeats the order back to the customer, which she'd do anyway, that serves as confirmation. Nothing gets clicked.

What if the best way for an AI to be useful is to disappear?
