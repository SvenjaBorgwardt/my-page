I've been spending a lot of time on a problem that might sound simple but isn't: Large language models are trained to be helpful, and in the context of learning, this is not always what is best for the person. That's why I started fine-tuning models to realign what helpful means when someone is trying to learn.

→ What follows: a contact sheet of the work that came out of that question. Three projects, two built tools, and the other parts — a violin, a regulator, a sail, a board.

Feature · N° 02

Kaleido — on feedback that lands

Feb 2025
→ current
Mistral · Python

Kaleido is a tool for giving individual feedback on writing. It came out of a frustration I had no answer for: with hundreds of papers to read each week, proper individual feedback simply wasn't possible.

A paper comes in. The name is stripped locally and replaced with a code. An anonymisation agent removes every remaining personal detail. A set of agents analyses the work along separate dimensions — structure, evidence, language, coherence, grammar, content. A final agent writes the feedback, addressed to the writer rather than about them.

"The first time I handed back Kaleido feedback, something happened I hadn't seen before. People came back with questions about their own essays. Not about the mark."

I called it Kaleido because a kaleidoscope turns the same fragments into a different pattern every time you look. That's what I want feedback to do: take a piece of writing and let its author see it from an angle they couldn't see on their own.

Feature · N° 03

Compass — on direction

Jun 2025
→ current
Mistral 7B · LoRA · DPO

Watching how people use AI, I keep noticing one thing: they tend to take the path of least resistance. In learning contexts, that worries me. AI creates a specific failure of self-assessment: a writer produces a good output and reads the quality of the output as evidence of their own learning. Cognitive science calls this the illusion of fluency.

For something to be learned, it has to be slightly harder than easy — Robert Bjork's desirable difficulties. Socratic agents are the obvious counter-move; they annoy actual learners. I'm fine-tuning a smaller model to sit between those two poles.

A compass points the way. It doesn't hand you the map.

Feature · N° 04

UTE — an AI that disappears

Nov 2025
BÄKO Hackathon
1st place

A voice-first ordering system for bakery counters. I built the first version at the BÄKO Hackathon with three other women, and we won first place.

Germany's local bakery is a place of conversation. The assistant knows your family, your allergies, the cake last week was for your daughter. Then the order has to go into the register, and the conversation breaks.

UTE handles the admin so the conversation doesn't have to stop. Listens while the assistant talks, transcribes in real time, matches items against the catalogue, builds the order in the background. When the assistant repeats the order back — which she'd do anyway — that serves as confirmation. Nothing gets clicked.

What if the best way for an AI to be useful is to disappear?
Etymology

UTE — Unkomplizierte Theken-Eingabe. Uncomplicated counter input. Also a common German name — a presence, not a system.

Built with

Three other women. One weekend. One patient baker. Real-time STT + catalogue matching.
