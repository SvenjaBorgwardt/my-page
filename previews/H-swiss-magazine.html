Kaleido, or how feedback came back addressed.

A tool for giving individual feedback on student writing — the thing I had no answer for, with 240 students a week.

A student hands in their paper. Their name is stripped out locally and replaced with a code. An anonymisation agent removes every remaining personal detail from the text. A set of agents then analyses the work along separate dimensions: structure, evidence, language, coherence, grammar, content.

A final agent writes the feedback, addressed to the student rather than about them. The output is an HTML page they can read on their phone, with a progress view that shows how their writing has developed over the year.

Kaleido works well for English and Economics because I trained the agents on my specific types of tasks. Publishing a version that fits other subjects is on my list.

It began as a simple pipeline and grew. I added plagiarism detection, student progress analytics — all the things that seemed useful in theory — before subtracting most of it again. The subtraction was the hard part.

Etymology

Kaleido. A kaleidoscope turns the same fragments into a different pattern every time you look. That's what I want feedback to do: take a student's writing and let them see it from an angle they couldn't see on their own.

Data residency

Mistral, because EU jurisdiction matters when you're handling German school data.

Compass, or the trouble with the easy answer.

On the illusion of fluency, desirable difficulties, and what you do when Socratic agents stop being useful.

Watching how people use AI, I keep noticing one thing: people tend to take the path of least resistance. In learning contexts, that worries me. AI creates a specific failure of self-assessment: the student produces a good output and reads the quality of the output as evidence of their own learning.

Cognitive science calls this the illusion of fluency, the gap between how something feels to process and whether any of it has actually stuck. For something to be learned, it has to be slightly harder than easy, which is the idea behind Robert Bjork's desirable difficulties.

Socratic agents are the obvious counter-move. I've tried them, and if you just want to understand a concept, getting questions back is annoying. So I started building a tool that removes the AI's ability to give the answer directly — the interaction layer never sees the whole task. But the better the models got, the harder that became.

So I came to the conclusion that I had to fine-tune the model itself. Right now I'm using Compass to fine-tune Mistral 7B with LoRA and DPO. The goal is to make it genuinely useful for my English classes — specifically for debating.

UTE, or the AI that chose to disappear.

Germany's bakery is a place of conversation. Then the order goes into the register, and the conversation breaks. UTE stops that from happening.

UTE is a voice-first ordering system for bakery counters. I built the first version at the BÄKO Hackathon with three other women, and we won first place. Germany has a long tradition in bakery handwerk, the craft of the trained master baker. It's one of the last disciplines where the supermarket chains can't really compete, because the local bakery is something people are proud of.

The assistant knows your family, your allergies, the cake last week was for your daughter. What holds these shops together is conversation. Then the order has to go into the register, and the conversation breaks. The warmth is gone, interrupted by technology.

UTE listens while the assistant talks, transcribes in real time, matches items against the bakery's catalogue, and builds the order in the background. When the assistant repeats the order back to the customer, which she'd do anyway, that serves as confirmation. Nothing gets clicked.

Name

UTE. Unkomplizierte Theken-Eingabe. Uncomplicated counter input. Also a common German name — which fit what we wanted: a presence at the counter, not a system.

Why a weekend was enough

I'd already spent months on the same structure in a different context — a voice pipeline that listens, transcribes, matches, summarises. Point it at a bakery counter, and you get UTE.

What if the best way for an AI to be useful is to disappear?

§ II · DEPARTMENT

Also built, still mine.

Multi-agent · Mac Mini

Claudia

My multi-agent infrastructure. She runs locally on a Mac Mini that's always on in my flat. By day she orchestrates the agents behind Kaleido. The rest of the time she runs my home automation. Same framework, different endpoints. I named her Claudia because "the multi-agent framework on the Mac Mini" stopped being charming around the tenth mention. Most of what I know about agent design I learned by building her — and watching her break in interesting ways.

Market economics

Investor Game

A platform where participants develop product ideas, pitch them to a group, and invest play money into each other's pitches. Scoring is part quality-of-pitch, part performance-of-portfolio. You're rewarded both for being a good founder and for being a good judge of other founders. I wrote it because nothing off the shelf handled that combination. Deployed in a market economics class where I've never seen the topic taken more seriously.
