Why I built this

I'm Svenja and I teach English and Economics at a vocational school, and I built this for my students. I want to teach them how to debate in English, and I wanted every single student to get individual support while they learn. So I built this Dojo.

I wanted to give each of my students their own sensei that helps them go through each learning step and prepares them for real debates they have with their classmates. After each kata they immediately practise what they have learned and go into the dojo with real classmates. The sensei is always there to help but he will never just give you the answer. He is there to help when you are stuck, but he is handcuffed to not give the answer.

To make this possible for 30 students at the same time I built myself a dashboard where I can see live where every student struggles and where they thrive, so I can concentrate on the students that struggle and help out where they are stuck. My students love this so much that most of the time I just sit back and watch some really heated debates about if pineapple on pizza is a good choice, if AI agents should be included in political decisions and if ads should be allowed to play with your emotions.

Each belt teaches something new. In the beginning, how an argument is structured, then the fallacies, and in the end they will face the final boss: a tournament among several classes, with a winner who will be remembered forever.

Belt guide

Inside each belt

BELT 01 立 Stance Live in class

The moveBuild one argument that actually lands — in three sentences.

What is learned

  • Claim — take a clear side: say what should happen.
  • Reason — give the why behind your claim.
  • Impact — say why it matters, who wins what.

Each concept comes with a worked example, a plain-language "how do I find it" rule, and two exercises before the next one starts.

The fight

  • Motion on screen, e.g. "Schools should replace exams with project work."
  • Students pair up, pick sides: for or against.
  • Laptops closed — one live round, 2:00 on the timer.
  • After: 5 minutes to rewrite the argument, stronger.
First time most of them ever defend a position out loud — with a structure to hold on to.
BELT 02 破 Clash Live in class

The moveSpot a trick argument, call it by name, and hit back with a real counter.

What is learned

  • Straw Man — changes your point into something extreme, then attacks that.
  • Ad Hominem — attacks who you are instead of what you said.
  • Appeal to Popularity — says it must be right because many people agree.

Then the counter-move: name the trick, restore your real point, answer the argument that was actually made.

The fight

  • Three rounds of 2:00 each, roles switch every round.
  • Role cards: one student secretly plays a fallacy, the other must catch it live.
  • The model sentence: "That is a Straw Man. I never said that. My real point is…"
  • After: rewrite the rebuttal, fallacy named.
The reference bar with all three fallacies stays visible — nobody has to fight from memory alone.
BELT 03 構 Case In design

The moveThe combo: chain three arguments into one full speech.

What is learned

  • One argument is not enough anymore: build three Claim–Reason–Impact arguments on one motion.
  • Order them: strongest where it counts, signposts between them.
  • Open and close so the whole room follows — and remembers.

The fight

  • Same rhythm: train the case with the sensei, then deliver it live, device closed.
  • The partner answers with their own case — first full speech against full speech.
Everything from Belts 1 and 2 stays in play: solid arguments, and the blocks against trick replies.
BELT 04 適 Adapt In design

The moveThe difficulty spike: keep your thread when the ground shifts.

What is learned

  • Handle the interruption: someone cuts in mid-speech with a question or challenge.
  • React, stay cool, answer — and find the way back to your own line of argument.
  • Decide live: take the point, deflect it, or turn it around.

The fight

  • The partner's job is to interrupt — at the worst possible moment.
  • The speaker's job: keep fighting without losing the thread.
This is the belt where debating starts to feel like the real thing: live, unpredictable, and survivable.
BELT 05 闘 Tournament In design

The moveThe final boss: everything at once, under real pressure.

What is learned

  • Fast preparation: the topic is revealed only 15 minutes before the round.
  • Build a case, expect the tricks, plan for interruptions — all four moves together.

The fight

  • Bracket format: the whole dojo watches every round.
  • At the end there is one winner — who will be remembered forever.
The tournament is the unplugged test of the whole curriculum: no sensei in the ring, only what the student can do now.

Let me tell you what this place is. In this Dojo I will teach you how to debate. Debating means you say what you think, you give good reasons for it, and you stay calm and fair while you speak. Maybe that sounds big right now. But it is a skill, and like every skill, you can learn it, one small step at a time. And you will use it everywhere: in school, at work, with your friends and your family, for the rest of your life. A person who can explain their ideas gets heard.

Here is our path, so you always know where you are. First, I will show you how a strong argument is built, piece by piece, so you can see what each part does. Then we practice together, slowly, until it feels natural. And when you are ready, you will step into the ring and debate with a real classmate, while I stand at your side.

Are you ready? Then let us begin.

Step 1 of 3 · Learn the move

First, a short lesson.

Every round starts with a small lesson on screen. Belt 1 is about taking a clear stance.

Claim what you believe. Ask yourself: what should happen? Say it in one clear sentence.
Reason why you believe it. Ask yourself: why is this true?
Impact why it matters. Ask yourself: what changes if we do this? Picture it.

See the move

Here is a full argument, the way a student would say it. It sounds like one thought. It is built from three parts.

School should start at nine.

Claim

This part takes a side. You can agree or disagree with it. That is what makes it a claim.

Teenagers fall asleep late because their body clock shifts.

Reason

This part tells us why the claim is true. A claim without a reason is just an opinion.

A class that is half asleep learns half as much.

Impact

This part shows what is at stake. It is the part that makes people care.

Spot the gap

Our canteen needs more vegetarian days, because growing meat burns huge amounts of energy.

One part is missing. Which one?

Almost. Look at the start again: “Our canteen needs more vegetarian days” takes a clear side. The claim is there. One of the other two parts is the quiet one.

Almost. The word “because” is doing its work: the energy point is the reason. Look for the part that shows why all of this matters.

Impact Yes. The argument takes a side and gives a reason, and then it stops. It never shows what is at stake. Picture the school after one year of veggie days, and suddenly it matters.

Built the way the belts are built: the worked example comes first, the check uses a fresh example, and you recall it within a minute. Teachers know this as explicit instruction, worked examples and retrieval practice.
